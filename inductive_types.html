Types of Inductive Reasoning

There are five different types of inductive reasoning. These are inductive generalizations, statistical induction, analogical induction, predictive induction, and casual interference. Arguments in these types of inductive reasoning vary in strength and weakness.

1. Inductive Generalization

Inductive Generalization refers to creating generalized statements about a whole population.

Example

  • All of the flamingos I saw were pink.
  • Therefore, all flamingos are pink.

2. Statistical Induction

Statistical Induction uses statistical data to make generalized statements.

Example

  • 90% of the flamingos I have seen around the world are pink.
  • Therefore, 90% of the world's flamingos are pink

3. Analogical Induction

Analogical Induction indicates that two groups are likely to further share qualities based on their similarities.

Example

  • Flamingos look like ostriches.
  • Ostriches lay eggs.
  • Therefore, flamingos also lay eggs.

4. Predictive Induction

Predictive Induction makes a prediction about the future based on information from the past.

Example

  • Flamingos always lay eggs every year.
  • Therefore, flamingos will lay eggs this year.

5. Causal Interference

Causal Interference uses inductive logic to establish a causal relationship between a premise and a conclusion.

Example

  • In the summer, there are flamingos in the lake.
  • Therefore, summer will bring flamingos to the lake.
